Adam joined the Tivoli Storage design group in late 2011 as a Senior Information Architect. He was an individual contributor working on Tivoli Operations Center, a new storage administration solution designed with modern front end programming languages and using OneUI language (pre IBM Design Language). After three months he was recognized for leadership skills that demonstrated both mentoring and design culture. He became a UX Team Lead in early 2013. His understanding of how to integrate design from an operational and practice standpoint in large corporations enabled him to organize and put practices in place to keep the growing Storage UX team aligned and focused. Adam also met with customers through user groups and workshops to understand their perspective as well as working environments in order to affect both strategy, vision and execution. He became involved with the early development of Service Engage on DeveloperWorks where SaaS solutions would be offered for sale and he worked cross discipline to define a user experience model to connect IBM.com to DeveloperWorks. Leading the effort to secure the Storage area to enter the Hallmark program he led a team that focused on simplifying the way clients access storage server environments through File Restore, a self-serve web-based portal for line-of-business users to easily recover lost or corrupt files without the intervention of a system administrator released in 2014 to the market.

Adam joined IBM Systems Strategy & Transformation team in 2014 to bring his design eminence as IBM Systems Design Lead to interface with Power, zSystems and Storage Design Champions, evangelize the use of IBM Design Thinking practices with product teams and help with active Hallmark Projects by coaching and mentoring offering teams over time to increase their confidence and competence in IBM Design Thinking.

Adam led the definition of the project, design research, facilitated a key workshop with IBMers and sponsor users, defined a UX model.

Due to our work for SmartCare, the Strategy & Development Design Team became involved with the important IBM Client Support Transformation initiative through Robert McDonald. Starting with envisioning and prototyping a self-help support capability called Guided Support within IBM Support Portal Beta, it leverages IBM Care, Watson Alchemy (natural language processing) and Asset Reuse Manager through short query conversation. Adam is now leading design efforts in taking IBM Design incubator Project Opel recommendations and driving its implementation. Adam has engaged with end-customers and IBM Partners in venues such as Tivoli User Groups and IBM conferences. He has also led many workshops around IBM Design Thinking and Agile for IBM product teams, with sponsor user participation.

As IBM transforms into a cognitive company supported by IBM Design Thinking with Agile culture, the intersection of belief and demonstrable value looms over everything we create. Our motto may be âThink,â but it is more than just a word silkscreened on our shirts. Coined by Thomas Watson in 1911, his intent was for us to âThinkâ by widening our thoughts and focusing our minds to transform old ways of doing business into new by bringing fresh positivity and ground-breaking solutions to the world and markets we serve. To truly widen and focus our perspectives and enhance our products and services, we must accept both the power and pain of transformation, as well as the inherent element of risk that it brings.

For simple problems where incremental change occurs, the future seems easy to understand. Yet, for more complex problems with many interdependencies and unknowns, the act of designing can easily fall apart. When designing the ânewâ, there is little precedent and many evolving opportunities, unknowns, and constraints. John Chris Jones, the British industrial designer who wrote the book Design Methods in 1970 saw the challenge of design:

âThe act of designing is difficult because designers are given problems to solve by using current information to predict a future state that will not come about unless their predictions are correct.â

As designers, we aim to radically collaborate with users and specific disciplines to observe and ask the right questions about ourselves, our solutions and our markets (âwidening our thoughtsâ). We reflect and identify hurdles that impede value and deter innovation (âfocusing our mindsâ). We prototype in order to learn, become wiser and more confident through iteration and focus in order to create transformative, iconic outcomes (âtransform old ways of doing businessâ). To create truly relevant solutions, it is critical we approach this process with as little prejudice or personal agenda as possible. We also need to be mindful of how transformations can expose us, and our clients, to risk and how to capitalize on or mitigate risk as needed.

Simply put, risk is some level of probability of negative exposure which may negate the level of utility we want to achieve. Some negative probabilities have a high chance of happening and some have a low to nonexistent chance. Translated into finance, stocks are riskier than bonds, but generate higher returns over long periods. Response to risk is not universal. People generally tend to be naturally risk adverse since we donât like to sacrifice what we think we currently have for the possibility of something better, even for possible eventual gain. So people will either respond to risk through fear and retreat, while others will respond with curiosity and acceptance. But both will try to understand and mitigate negative exposure to risk.

Common types of risks :
  • bias and stereotypes (people to people or individuals/team to assumptions)
  • people to values (lack of values or shared mental models and culture)
  • people to people (distrust or power plays)
  • people to skills (lack of skills)
  • people to time (lack of urgency or the ability to act in time)
  • where the new meets established processes, procedures and protocols (âwe donât do it that wayâ)
  • missing market cycles (too early or late)
Despite striking fear into our hearts by its association with failure, shortcoming and potential humiliation, risk is not inherently negative. Over my career as a visual designer, identity systems designer, brand strategist, information architect and UX Lead and UX Manager, risk has always been with me. At first risk made me fearful as I did not know how to respond to it â sort of like a bad horror film where you see shadow and no form. It seemed out of my control to do anything about it (and usually it was).

As I became more seasoned and began to collaborate with other roles that impacted my work, risk became something to manage. By talking about risk and what it means, it helped determine the level of actual exposure to a work item, a deadline â or even the goal of a project. As I began to lead teams and work on problems where there was little precedent, risk became an old friend, not to fear but to embrace and deal with. In Mandarin the symbol for crisis is a combination of danger (crisis/risk) and opportunity. Opportunity is associated with a potentially risky exploration in which all an individual or company has is a hypothesis and bits of experience projected onto a desired outcome. We need to ask ourselves about these variables:
  • why is this goal important to me/us?
  • what do we know?
  • what would we like to find out?
Agile culture realizes the positive element of risk and exhorts the creed to âfail early and fastâ where we iteratively learn to understand what doesnât work, isolating and iterating possible solutions until a team achieves the desired result. IBMâs growth businesses were born in this millennium and a culture of uncertainty, ambiguity and risk has been built in. However, in our decades-old core businesses this transformation is proving far more elusive. To innovate and create meaningful change interrupts the status quo, which is often well-entrenched after years of stability. Shaking things up can be unnerving, but it is necessary for transformation change.

Because design is associated with exploring without constraint, a fourth variable may also arise: discovering things we did not know we didnât know. There is a psychology and sociology around exploration. Ideally, designers seek to understand stakeholdersâ articulated needs and examine them against what their actual environments and behaviors tells us. The goal is to predict and balance their immediate needs and desires with those of their foreseeable future. âPredictâ is the operative word. It can expose a designer or design team to levels of negative risk. Whether viewed as a transformative opportunity or a disaster depends largely on the environment in which designers work. If the culture is open to embracing ambiguity and unknown factors, realizing the value of a new variable is great. If the environment is punitive and risk-adverse, designers will be less likely to act on these opportunities and the potential reward will be lost.

There are pitfalls lurking for an explorer/designer. One is âmagical thinking.â This involves creating causal relationships between actions and events not justified by reason and observation, but by personal bias or preconceived ideas. Bordering on fallacy, these relationships are often imagined to will a theory into reality through brute force. Based on bias and poor observations, they often result in the worst possible outcome â negative results and experiences for our clients.
